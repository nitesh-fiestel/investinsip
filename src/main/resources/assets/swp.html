What is a Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)?

A Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP) is a facility provided by mutual funds that allows an investor to withdraw a fixed or variable amount from their investment at pre-determined intervals (monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, or annually). It is the opposite of a SIP (Systematic Investment Plan).

SWPs are widely used by retirees to create a steady source of income from their accumulated retirement corpus. Instead of keeping money idle in a savings account, an SWP allows your corpus to stay invested in mutual funds (generating potentially higher returns) while providing you with regular cash flow.

How Does SWP Work?

When you set up an SWP, you instruct the mutual fund house to redeem a specific amount of units equivalent to the cash you need. For example:

  • If you have 10,000 units in a fund and the NAV is â‚¹20.
  • You want to withdraw â‚¹5,000.
  • The fund house will redeem 250 units (5000 / 20) and credit the amount to your bank account.
  • Your remaining units will be 9,750, which will continue to grow according to market performance.

Benefits of SWP

  • Regular Income Stream: Provides predictability and stability, essential for post-retirement expenses.
  • Tax Efficiency: SWPs are more tax-efficient than traditional Fixed Deposit interest. In an SWP, only the capital gains part of the withdrawal is taxed, not the entire principal.
  • Rupee Cost Averaging (in reverse): You redeem fewer units when markets are high and more units when markets are down, but since the goal is income, the focus remains on the cash flow.
  • Capital Growth: If your withdrawal rate (e.g., 6%) is lower than the fund's return rate (e.g., 10%), your corpus will actually grow over time despite withdrawals.

SWP vs Dividend Plan (IDCW)

Many investors choose the Dividend option (now called IDCW) for regular income. However, SWP is far more tax-efficient and reliable.

Feature SWP (Growth Option) Dividend (IDCW)
Certainty Fixed amount, guaranteed frequency Uncertain, depends on fund profits
Principal Protection High (if withdrawal < returns) NAV falls by dividend amount
Taxation Only gains taxed (Capital Gains) Entire amount taxed as Income

The Golden Rule: The Cool-Down Period

Starting an SWP immediately after retiring can be risky due to Sequence of Returns Risk. If the market crashes right when you start withdrawing, you are forced to sell more units at low prices, depleting your corpus much faster.

ðŸ’¡ Strategy: Keep 1 to 3 years of expenses in a liquid fund or Fixed Deposit (The Cool-Down Bucket).

If the market is down, withdraw from this "Cool-down Bucket" and let your equity corpus recover. If the market is up, you can withdraw from your equity returns. This simple buffer can extend the life of your retirement fund by several years!

Taxation on SWP

SWP withdrawals are treated as redemptions. The tax depends on the type of fund (Equity or Debt) and the holding period.

  • Equity Funds (Holding > 1 year): Gains up to â‚¹1 Lakh per financial year are tax-free. Gains above â‚¹1 Lakh are taxed at 10% (LTCG).
  • Equity Funds (Holding < 1 year): Taxed at 15% (STCG).
  • Debt Funds: Taxed as per your income tax slab (for investments made after April 1, 2023).
